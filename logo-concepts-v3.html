Concept 01

Euler Spiral

The Clothoid — where curvature κ = s (curvature equals arc length). The ONLY curve with this property. Used in highway and railway design because it creates the smoothest possible transition. Two mirrored spirals meet at the origin. Discovered by Euler, 1744.

Mathematical Formula

x(t) = ∫cos(πs²/2)ds, y(t) = ∫sin(πs²/2)ds (Fresnel integrals). The defining property: κ = s — curvature equals arc length. This creates the smoothest possible transition from straight to curved. Used in highway design, roller coasters, and font design (spiro curves).

Concept 02

Spira Mirabilis

The "miraculous spiral" — Jakob Bernoulli's name for the logarithmic spiral. The ONLY curve that maintains its shape at any scale (self-similarity). Found in nautilus shells, spiral galaxies, hurricanes, and sunflower seeds. Nature's universal pattern.

Mathematical Formula

r = ae^(bθ) in polar coordinates. Key property: self-similarity — scaling by any factor produces the same shape, just rotated. Bernoulli loved it so much he requested it on his tombstone with "Eadem mutata resurgo" (Though changed, I arise the same). The golden spiral uses b = ln(φ)/(π/2).

Concept 03

Brachistochrone

The cycloid — curve traced by a point on a rolling circle. The solution to the Brachistochrone problem: the path of FASTEST descent under gravity. Also the Tautochrone: same descent time from any starting point. Challenged Newton, Leibniz, and the Bernoullis.

Mathematical Formula

x = r(θ - sinθ), y = r(1 - cosθ). The Brachistochrone (Greek: "shortest time") proved by Johann Bernoulli in 1696. Famously challenged the greatest mathematicians of the era. Newton solved it overnight. Demonstrates that the shortest path isn't always the fastest.

Concept 04

Catenary

The "chain curve" — the shape of a perfectly flexible chain hanging under gravity. Minimizes potential energy. Used by Gaudí for the Sagrada Família (inverted for arches), and shapes the Gateway Arch. Natural equilibrium made visible.

Concept 05

Harmonic Wave

A continuous sine wave wrapped into a circle — representing the fundamental oscillation of nature. Sound, light, and electromagnetic waves all follow this pattern. Two complete cycles create perfect symmetry. The mathematics of vibration made visible.

Geometric Principle

Based on r = a + b·sin(nθ) — a sinusoidal modulation of a circle. With n=4 peaks, this creates four-fold symmetry. The sine wave is the fundamental building block of Fourier analysis — any complex signal can be decomposed into sine waves. The language of physics and signal processing.

Concept 06

Möbius Ring

A simplified, bold interpretation of the Möbius strip — a surface with only one side. Two curves that appear separate but are actually one continuous path. Represents the seamless, infinite loop of autonomous agent operation.

Geometric Principle

The Möbius strip has Euler characteristic χ = 0. Represented here as two complementary bezier curves. The twist creates depth from 2D. Symbolizes continuity without beginning or end — perfect for autonomous systems.

Concept 07

Cassini Oval

The locus of points where the PRODUCT of distances to two foci is constant. Unlike an ellipse (where the SUM is constant), this creates far more beautiful curves — from smooth ovals to figure-8s to separate loops. Discovered by Giovanni Cassini in 1680 while studying planetary orbits.

Mathematical Formula

|PF₁| · |PF₂| = b² — product of distances to two foci. In polar: r⁴ - 2a²r²cos(2θ) + a⁴ = b⁴. When b=a, creates the Lemniscate of Bernoulli. Cassini proposed these curves for planetary orbits before Kepler's ellipses were accepted. The curves change shape beautifully as b/a varies.

Concept 08

Limaçon

The "snail curve" — discovered by Étienne Pascal (Blaise Pascal's father) in 1637. Created by tracing a point on a circle rolling around another circle. Special cases include the cardioid (when a=b) and the trisectrix. The inner loop appears when b > a. Pure mathematical poetry.

Mathematical Formula

r = a + b·cos(θ) in polar coordinates. When b > a, the curve has an inner loop. When a = b, it becomes a cardioid. The name comes from Latin "limax" (snail). Étienne Pascal discovered it while studying conchoids. Also used to solve the angle trisection problem (trisectrix of Limaçon).
